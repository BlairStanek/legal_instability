Jordan Harris previously served as a highest officer in a private group called The Torch Circle in Louisiana. On one occasion, the Government charged him under 18 U.S.C. § 922(g) for unlawful firearm possession. Soon after his arraignment, Jordan Harris was granted bond and signed a written notice warning him of possible additional penalties if he committed further offenses while on release.

 While released pending his sentence on the firearm offense, Jordan Harris and others planned to carry out acts of intimidation by setting objects on fire in various parts of Louisiana. On the night Jordan Harris began serving his prison term for the firearm conviction, his associates proceeded with those acts at multiple locations in the state. The Government subsequently filed separate charges against him, including conspiracy to interfere with federal rights under 18 U.S.C. § 241, use of fire in commission of a felony under 18 U.S.C. § 844(h), interference with certain housing rights under 42 U.S.C. § 3631(a), interference involving public schooling under 18 U.S.C. § 245(b)(2)(A), and conspiracy to make a false declaration under 18 U.S.C. § 1623.

 Jordan Harris entered a guilty plea on most counts. During the plea hearing, the district court stated he might face up to 18 years in prison. Under 18 U.S.C. § 3147, however, the Government later requested an additional penalty for offenses committed while on release, raising the possible maximum prison time well beyond what Jordan Harris had initially been told. Jordan Harris then argued he had not been made fully aware at the time of his plea of how much additional sentencing exposure he faced under the law.

 The Government asserts that Jordan Harris received sufficient formal notification of potential sentencing consequences, pointing to documents he signed outlining the possibility of enhanced punishment for new crimes while on bond. The Government underscores that Rule 11 of the Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure does not demand mechanically perfect warnings if, in practice, the defendant has a fair understanding of the nature and potential punishment of the charges. The Government further cites decisions interpreting Rule 11 to allow courts to overlook certain procedural variances if the information possessed by the defendant, coupled with any advisory warnings given, shows he would still have entered the plea under the same circumstances. According to the Government, the statutory references in place, the written release notice, and the advisements given in court should satisfy the requirement to ensure a well-informed plea, and the relevant sentencing statutes—such as 18 U.S.C. § 3147—do not mandate a new plea if the court’s process, on the whole, conveyed the seriousness and scope of potential penalties.

 Jordan Harris contends he was never given accurate information concerning the maximum possible sentence he faced under 18 U.S.C. § 3147. He points to Rule 11 requirements, arguing they recognize a defendant’s right to weigh the consequences of entering a guilty plea with knowledge of all substantially potential sanctions. In his view, a plea is only valid if it is knowing and voluntary; lacking knowledge of a significantly greater sentencing exposure undermines that principle. He relies on cases emphasizing that a substantial difference between what a court warns and what the law actually provides can materially affect a defendant’s decision to plead. Because he was told a maximum of 18 years at the plea hearing but then learned that the total maximum could rise considerably due to the enhancement, Jordan Harris maintains that he was deprived of critical information that might have altered his willingness to plead guilty under the same terms.
