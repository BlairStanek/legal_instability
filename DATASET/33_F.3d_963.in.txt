This matter involves a dispute arising from federal Aid to Families with Dependent Children (AFDC) rules limiting the allowable resource value of an automobile. Lee Bates, who resides in Iowa and cares for a minor child, had her AFDC benefits terminated due to her vehicle equity exceeding a specified limit. She contends that her automobile is essential for traveling to multiple jobs, especially given the limited public transportation in her rural area. The Secretary, relying on authority granted by federal legislation, set the automobile equity limit as part of a broader effort to restrict AFDC eligibility to those considered the neediest households.

 The limit in question, established in the early 1980s, finds its origins in changes enacted by the Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act of 1981 (OBRA). OBRA mandated stricter overall resource ceilings for AFDC recipients, and it authorized the Secretary to create a specific cap for automobile ownership. In formulating the cap, the Secretary’s staff used data from a national survey originally conducted for the Food Stamp program. That survey aimed to gauge typical household assets, including automobile equity. Although it did not specifically target individuals solely receiving AFDC, it was used as a proxy because of the significant overlap between households receiving both AFDC and Food Stamp benefits.

 According to Lee Bates, the current $1,500 equity limit fails to reflect her economic reality. She asserts that this figure, established decades ago, is outdated given the increased cost of reliable transportation since the regulation’s adoption. She points to her situation as emblematic of how families with higher equity in a dependable vehicle face disqualification from AFDC. The Secretary counters that this specific limit reflects a legislative mandate to conserve federal resources and target assistance more narrowly, asserting that the underlying data reasonably supported the cap. Both sides emphasize the importance of balancing the AFDC program’s goal of promoting self-sufficiency with the need to ensure that federal funds go only to households deemed most in need.

 In support of the Secretary’s position, it is argued that the governing statute, 42 U.S.C. § 602(a)(7)(B), explicitly grants broad discretion to set the automobile equity limit. Proponents of this view say that, under precedents like Chevron U.S.A., Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., such administrative decisions are entitled to substantial deference if they are not arbitrary or capricious. They cite the Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act of 1981 and legislative materials indicating Congress’s objective of containing expenditures by excluding resources above a certain threshold. They add that it was reasonable to rely on a then-current national survey—the only systematically gathered data on automobile equity available at the time—to set an appropriate figure. They further contend that neither the statute nor other federal law mandates inflationary adjustments, and Congress, which could have required or even suggested such updates, has not done so.

 Lee Bates, for her part, maintains that under the Administrative Procedure Act, 5 U.S.C. § 706, the Secretary’s actions must rest on a rational connection between the facts found and the choice made. She submits that using a Food Stamp survey from the 1970s fails to account adequately for the distinct needs and circumstances of AFDC families, particularly in today’s economy. Her position argues that the stated statutory goal of aiding needy families to achieve self-sufficiency is undermined when beneficiaries are forced to relinquish reliable transportation essential for working or seeking employment. Additionally, Bates contends that the Secretary’s reliance on data long predating current economic conditions is no longer valid, making the limit arbitrary and capricious. She further cites the absence of any formal reexamination or revision of the automobile limit despite substantially changed financial realities, asserting that without adjustments to reflect inflation or cost trends, the original rationale for the cap no longer holds.
