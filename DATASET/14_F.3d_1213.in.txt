In this matter, Jeremiah Gray had multiple convictions for burglary in Minnesota. Each burglary conviction was considered a violent felony under federal standards. After serving sentences for these offenses in a correctional facility, Gray received official documents upon release purporting to restore certain civil rights. Questions subsequently arose about whether these convictions should be taken into account for a federal statute imposing a 15-year minimum penalty, which depends on the existence of three previous convictions for a violent felony or a serious drug offense.

 Gray’s concurrent sentences for three burglary convictions ended in 1986. Upon completion, he received a discharge certificate that restored his civil rights and stated that it did not affect any restrictions imposed under a “Federal Gun Control Act.” Later, Gray was again incarcerated, and upon release in 1990, he received another certificate from the state that omitted the earlier note about federal firearms disabilities. In both instances, state law at the time included various firearm restrictions, such as limits on the possession of pistols and, later, a 10-year bar on possessing any firearm following release from custody for a violent offense.

 While Gray claimed that the discharge certificates effectively erased or neutralized his prior convictions for purposes of federal law, there was disagreement over whether the certificate language (or state statutes alone) placed him on sufficient notice that firearm possession remained restricted. The discussion turned on the interaction between the wording in 18 U.S.C. § 921(a)(20), which addresses situations where a person’s civil rights have been restored, and whether the certificates expressly limited Gray’s right to own or handle firearms.

 For Gray, the strongest arguments in favor of disregarding the earlier convictions under the federal enhancement statute centered on the language of 18 U.S.C. § 921(a)(20). Under that provision, if a state restores a person’s civil rights, federal law generally does not count that conviction unless the restoration document itself expressly states that the person is prohibited from possessing firearms. Gray underscored that his certificates announced a restoration of civil rights “the same as if such conviction had not taken place,” and did not specifically tell him he was forbidden to “ship, transport, possess, or receive firearms.” His position further relied on precedents interpreting 18 U.S.C. § 921(a)(20) to require an express limitation when civil rights are restored through official documentation. Gray maintained that silence or vague references to “federal law” did not rise to an explicit notice. He also pointed out that a separate state restriction on pistols or firearms did not suffice if the restoration paperwork did not itself set forth a clearly stated prohibition.

 On the other side, the United States pointed to the very presence of state law provisions barring firearm possession for a certain period after release and to the language in one of Gray’s discharge certificates noting a federal firearms disability. Officials argued that such statements or references serve as adequate notice, especially because individuals receiving certificates are put on notice there may be ongoing restrictions. Moreover, the United States maintained that multiple burglary convictions, even if concurrent, were distinct criminal episodes that could each be counted under 18 U.S.C. § 924(e)(1). The United States further relied on interpretations that reading all relevant state statutes together could supply the express notice required, emphasizing that no restoration could be considered complete if state law retained any firearms restrictions on the individual and those restrictions had been clearly enacted.
